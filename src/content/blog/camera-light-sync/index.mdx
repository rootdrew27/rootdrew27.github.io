Modern cameras are designed to handle the flicker of a typical LED, but a non-standard flicker and a high-intensity makes recording under these LEDs challenging.

## Background

It is well-known that LEDs flicker, and while anti-flicker technology is built into many modern cameras, it is designed to handle the flicker that results from LEDs powered by [mains power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mains_electricity). For my use case, the LED light is driven by [pulse width modulation](https://www.sameskydevices.com/blog/pulse-width-modulation-pwm-how-it-works-and-why-its-essential-in-electronics), and importantly, its flicker frequency differs from the standard 50/60 Hz of mains power. 

### Capture Scenario

The important characteristics of my capture scenario are:
1. No Control Over Light Source
2. High-Intenstiy Light (13k-20k+ lux)
3. Non-Standard Flicker Frequency (not 50/60 Hz)
4. Color Multiplexing (4 color channels)

Let's look at each one...

#### No Control over the Light Source

When controlling the light source is a feasible approach, it makes synchronization of the camera and the light a much simpler task. Unfortunately, controlling the light source and/or adding a controlled light source was out of the question in the setting that I was engineering for.

#### High Intensity Light

The light is bright, 13,000 to 20,000+ lux, and my camera was close enough to oversaturate the sensor under any ordinary exposure. The conventional answer to a scene with this much light intensity is a camera with [high dynamic range]((https://www.bhphotovideo.com/explora/photography/hands-on-review/a-guide-to-neutral-density-filters)) (HDR), but depending on how it's implemented, HDR introduces its own drawbacks, cost among them.

#### Non-Standard Flicker Frequency

Knowing that my light source was driven by pulse width modulation and that its [switching frequency](https://www.sameskydevices.com/blog/pulse-width-modulation-pwm-how-it-works-and-why-its-essential-in-electronics#:~:text=right%20power%20level.-,What%20is%20Switching%20Frequency%3F,-Switching%20frequency%20is) was non-standard, I determined that syncing the camera's capture to the light would be beneficial, if not essential.

#### Color Multiplexing

The light I was capturing appears white, but a common technique known as color multiplexing was in use. Four color channels, each with their own duty cycle and offset were used by the light's manufacturer. To the eye, and to any exposure long enough to integrate across a full PWM cycle, the blend into a steady white.

## Solution

Neutral-density filters and a short exposure time tamed the brightness, but synchronizing the camera to the light was still necessary. With the light's internals inaccessible, synchronization had to come from its output. A phototransistor aimed at the light, together with a simple circuit, can convert the light's intensity into a voltage, producing a waveform representing the combined color channels.

From there, the goal is not merely to catch a bright portion of the waveform but to land every exposure at the *same phase* of the multiplexed cycle, so the same combination of channels is captured frame after frame; drift in phase alters the color drastically, while a consistent phase holds it steady. A microcontroller running custom software samples the recovered signal and drives the camera's trigger pin to do exactly that. The second video below shows the result: stable color and brightness, with no pulsing or partially-lit frames, and as bonuses, no motion blur (due to global shutter and short exposure) and no need for a costly HDR sensor.

## Conclusion

The solution detailed in this article is generalizable to different switching frequencies and differing waveforms, which is necessary for my application. Furthermore, due to its affordability, it may be applicable to a wider range of applications.
